WHY DO YOU HAVE TO CARE?

In this fast technological advancement era, your personal data really matter. You don't want someone to know how much you make in a year?, what is your address?, what your phone number is?, what is your browsing history?, who's your best friends? and a lot more sensitive information to get leaked into public.

Online privacy is important for numerous reasons. You don’t want to share details of your personal life with strangers and it’s hard to be sure what personal information is gathered and by whom: information collected by one company might be shared with another.

Your personal information is highly desireable to cyber criminals. After these people steals your data, they may be able to accsess your banking details, obtain credit card or loans, destroy your credit rating. After all that your information will be sold in the internet for 5$.

While many people repeat the “I have nothing to hide” mantra when it comes to their reasons for freely handing all of their data over to giant tech companies, governments, or the random developer behind a flashlight app for their phone, Lopp’s case shows the online privacy debate is much more complex than it may seem at first glance.

There are plenty of arguments to make against those who say they have nothing to hide, but even after everything from Edward Snowden’s NSA leaks to 143 million Americans’ social security numbers being compromised in the 2017 Equifax hack, the vast majority of people simply do not care enough to make changes to the way they use the internet. It’s convenience over security.

If you’re one of those people who doesn’t think much about online privacy or computer security, then perhaps you should take a listen to what Jameson Lopp has to say because online privacy is directly related to one’s own physical security in today’s digital world. In a recent interview with Epicenter, Lopp discussed the risks associated with poor online privacy practices.
